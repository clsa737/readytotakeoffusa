## The U.S. Airline Hiring Process

### What Recruiters, Chief Pilots & Assessors Evaluate

#### **Recruiters Focus On:**
- Resume completeness and professionalism
- Flight hours and experience quality
- Career progression and stability
- Communication skills and professionalism

#### **Chief Pilots & Assessors Evaluate:**
- Technical proficiency and decision-making
- Crew Resource Management (CRM) principles
- Leadership and communication
- Adaptability and problem-solving
- Professional presence and attitude

#### **Red Flags Across All Evaluators:**
- Attendance/punctuality issues
- Regulatory violations or safety incidents
- Negative references or attitude problems
- Gaps in logbook records or training
- Poor communication or presentation

### Instructor Notes
*Discuss real-world airline examples of candidates who excelled and those who were rejected. Share specific stories about what separated successful applicants. Reference actual CRM incidents and how airlines use behavioral assessment to predict performance.*

## Human Factors & CRM: Why Airlines Hire People, Not Just Flight Hours

### The Reality of Modern Airline Operations
Modern aircraft are highly automated. Technical flying is necessary but not sufficient. Airlines hire **people** because:

1. **Decision-making under pressure** determines safety outcomes
2. **Communication failures** cause accidents, not mechanical failures
3. **Teamwork and leadership** prevent incidents from becoming accidents
4. **Attitude and adaptability** predict long-term success

### CRM Framework
- **Communication**: Clear, timely, assertive
- **Leadership**: Supporting team objectives
- **Teamwork**: Collaboration and mutual support
- **Situational Awareness**: Understanding the complete picture
- **Decision-making**: Sound judgment under stress
- **Stress Management**: Maintaining performance

### Why This Matters in Interviews
Interviewers ask behavioral questions specifically to assess CRM competencies. They're listening for:
- How you handle disagreement
- How you prioritize safety
- How you communicate with authority figures
- How you respond to mistakes
- How you support team members

### Instructor Notes
*Share real incident reports (NTSB) that demonstrate how CRM breakdowns contribute to accidents. Discuss modern airline culture shift from command authority to collaborative decision-making. Use examples of how international pilots must adapt their communication style from military or other training backgrounds.*

## Building Your Career Story: Creating a Compelling Professional Narrative

### What is Your Career Story?
Your career story is the **coherent narrative** that explains:
- Why you became a pilot
- How you've grown and developed
- What drives your decision-making
- Why you're interested in this airline
- How your experience prepares you for success

### Why It Matters
- Interviewers want to understand your **motivation** and **stability**
- Your story demonstrates **self-awareness**
- It connects disparate experiences into a coherent whole
- It reveals your **values and character**

### Building Blocks
1. **Origin**: Why aviation? What inspired your journey?
2. **Development**: Key experiences that shaped you as a pilot
3. **Values**: What matters most to you professionally?
4. **Vision**: Where do you see yourself in 5-10 years?
5. **This Airline**: Why does this specific airline fit your story?

### Authentic vs. Memorized
- Your story should be **authentic** and **flexible**
- Not a script to recite verbatim
- Genuine passion is evident to experienced interviewers
- Allow personality to shine through professionalism

### Instructor Notes
*Have students develop their personal career story and practice telling it in 2-3 minutes. Discuss how international pilots should frame their training and experience positively. Address how to discuss career challenges or setbacks as learning experiences. Emphasize authenticity and confidence.*
